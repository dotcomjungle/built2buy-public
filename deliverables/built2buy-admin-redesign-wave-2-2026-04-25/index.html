Stakeholder Profiles — Who They Really Are

A stakeholder is a person, not an org-chart slot. These profiles describe each of the eleven roles as the actual humans who'll use the system: their context, their day, what they're trying to accomplish, what blocks them today. The new admin gets designed around these humans — not around the plugins they currently fight with.

Platform Operator Built2Buy super-admin Platform-side

Who they really are: A Built2Buy engineer or operations lead. Lives in browser tabs, terminal sessions, and monitoring dashboards. Bounces across 30+ tenant sites in a day. Identifies as someone who runs a multi-tenant platform, not as "an admin with more checkboxes."

A typical day: Slack pings about a customer's site, dependency upgrades, multi-tenant deploys, customer support tickets, occasional bug reproduction. Shifts between proactive ops and reactive firefighting.

Top jobs to be done

  • When a customer reports a bug, reproduce it without disrupting their site, so I can fix before they call back.
  • When I roll out a platform change, see which tenants are affected and in what state, so I don't break someone in production.
  • When a new customer onboards, provision their site in minutes not days, so my queue doesn't grow.

What they own

Everything in the Platform area. Multisite operations, dev tools, integrations, customer onboarding, fleet observability.

Not their job

Customer business decisions — pricing, marketing, hiring. Built2Buy operates the platform; customers run their business.

Success looks like

Customer support response time under 4 hours; zero unannounced outages; multi-tenant deploys land cleanly across the fleet.

Frustration today: Has to sudo into a tenant's WP admin to debug something that should be observable from a control plane.
Owner / Administrator The customer's top admin Customer-side

Who they really are: The customer's owner, often 50-65, came up through sales or operations rather than software. The buck-stops-here human. Final authority on margin, pricing policy, and role assignments.

A typical day: Mostly NOT in the admin. Logs in maybe twice a month — to check a dashboard, approve something, or because someone called them yelling. Spends most of their time on the phone, in meetings, or with customers.

Top jobs to be done

  • When I open the system Monday morning, see if the business is healthy in 30 seconds, so I can get on with my day.
  • When something is off (revenue dropped, AR is high, key customer went quiet), know who to ask, so I don't waste a meeting.
  • When asked to approve a price exception or discount, get context fast, so I'm not the bottleneck.

What they own

Everything in their company's stack. Final pricing authority. Role assignments. Site operations.

Not their job

Built2Buy platform infrastructure. Daily order processing. Catalog data entry.

Success looks like

Owner-facing dashboards drive 80%+ of their interaction; approval queues clear in <24 hours; no surprise expenses on the monthly close.

Frustration today: "I don't know what's going on in my own business without asking three people."
Sales Director Top of the customer's sales hierarchy Customer-side

Who they really are: At a $15M+ distributor, this is a real role. Works the phone with the team and the road with key accounts. Often a former top rep promoted up. Lives in dashboards and rep one-on-ones.

A typical day: Sales reports in the morning, rep coaching mid-day, customer escalations as they come, pipeline reviews in the afternoon. In meetings half the time.

Top jobs to be done

  • Know how the sales team is performing this week, by region and by rep, so I can coach early.
  • When a key account has a problem, get involved before it escalates to the Owner.
  • Approve customer-specific contract pricing without rubber-stamping.

What they own

All sales activity — orders, accounts, contract pricing, customer-service tickets. Read-only on catalog and marketing.

Not their job

Catalog data entry. Marketing content. Site operations. Bookkeeping.

Success looks like

Hits revenue targets quarter over quarter; rep retention >85%; pricing exceptions clear in <48 hours.

Frustration today: "I have to call my reps to find out what's happening with their accounts."
Regional Sales Manager also: Internal Sales Rep Customer-side

Who they really are: Manages a region's reps. Geographically scoped — sees only their region's accounts. Often was a top rep promoted up, still carries some key accounts themselves. Half-seller, half-coach.

A typical day: Splits time between selling (their key regional accounts) and managing (the reps in their region). Phone calls, ride-alongs, account reviews.

Top jobs to be done

  • When a rep in my region underperforms, see what's happening with their accounts, so I can coach or reassign.
  • Place an order for a regional key account I'm closing on the phone, fast.
  • Approve a quote/contract that's within my region's authority.

What they own

Everything in their region. Cannot see other regions' accounts, orders, or pricing.

Not their job

National strategy (Sales Director's). Catalog/marketing/site ops.

Success looks like

Region hits its targets; new reps ramp in <90 days; their region produces no escalations to Sales Director.

Frustration today: Spreadsheets and email threads instead of one source of truth for their region.
Phone Team Desk module · sees ALL accounts Customer-side

Who they really are: Inside, desk-based. Headset on, two monitors. Order entry and customer support combined into one role. Often the first human voice a buyer hears when they call in.

A typical day: Phone rings. A buyer wants an order, an answer, a quote, or a return. Process. Phone rings again. Steady volume.

Top jobs to be done

  • Place a buyer's order in under two minutes while they're on the phone.
  • When a buyer asks "where's my order," give a one-click answer.
  • Process a return without leaving the buyer on hold.

What they own

Full order entry across ALL accounts. Customer service tickets. Read on accounts, catalog, pricing.

Not their job

Setting prices. Configuring shipping. Anything in marketing/catalog/site ops.

Success looks like

Average call <5 minutes; first-call resolution >70%; no re-keying of customer or order data.

Frustration today: "I have to bounce between five plugins to do one order."
Field Sales Rep also: Outside Sales Rep · field module · assigned accounts only Customer-side

Who they really are: Outside, mobile. Truck cab, hotel lobby, customer's back office. Phone in one hand, laptop in the other (or just phone). Identifies as a salesperson who uses a tool, not as an administrator of a website.

A typical day: Drive, visit, sell, drive, visit, sell. Often at a customer site for 30 minutes, then on the move. Order entry happens at night from a hotel, or on the spot when the buyer is ready.

Top jobs to be done

  • When in front of a buyer, place their order on the spot without looking like an IT person.
  • Look up the buyer's last 10 orders before walking in, so I can re-up them.
  • See inventory on hand before promising a delivery date.

What they own

Their assigned accounts only — orders, account history, contract pricing, customer-service tickets for those accounts.

Not their job

Other reps' accounts. Anything in catalog/marketing/site ops/shipping config.

Success looks like

Order capture in <2 minutes on mobile; re-orders >50% of their volume; mobile becomes their primary tool.

Frustration today: WP admin doesn't work on a phone. Reps write orders down and enter them at night, costing accuracy and speed.
Marketing Coordinator v1 name: Marketing Manager Customer-side

Who they really are: At small-to-mid distributors, often part-time, outsourced, or the Owner's spouse working with a Fiverr graphic designer. At $15M+ might be a real full-time role with a coordinator title.

A typical day: Email blasts, banner refreshes, promo coordination, catalog photography, sometimes copy-writing. Often coordinating with a contractor or vendor on creative work.

Top jobs to be done

  • When a promotion runs, get it onto the site and to buyers in one motion.
  • When traffic spikes, see what's working so I can do more of it.
  • Manage MAP/IMAP policy without breaking anything.

What they own

The Website. Marketing & Analytics. Promotions and coupons. Read on accounts and catalog.

Not their job

Setting list prices (Owner / Sales Director). Placing orders. Configuring shipping. Site infrastructure.

Success looks like

Email send-to-conversion >2%; site traffic month-over-month growth; promo launches happen in <1 day from concept to live.

Frustration today: Marketing scattered across plugins; no single view of "is marketing working."
Catalog Manager also: Buyer / Category Manager Customer-side

Who they really are: Often the most spreadsheet-fluent person in the building. Sometimes the buyer/purchasing person who also negotiates with vendors. Heavy data work, eye for detail.

A typical day: Loading new products, fixing attribute errors, updating cost sheets, coordinating with vendors on availability. Bulk operations dominate.

Top jobs to be done

  • When new products arrive, add them in bulk without typing every field.
  • When a product changes (cost, image, availability), update one place and have it reflected everywhere.
  • When a buyer or rep complains a product page is wrong, find and fix it fast.

What they own

The Catalog. Cost loading on products. Read on Website, Marketing, Returns.

Not their job

Setting sell prices (Owner/Sales Director). Marketing campaigns. Order processing. Anything customer-facing.

Success looks like

Product entry time per SKU <2 min; zero stale data complaints; cost margins always traceable.

Frustration today: Cost loaded in one place, sell price in another, with no consistency or single view.
Order Processor Office side Customer-side

Who they really are: Office-based, eyes on screen, processing orders, generating invoices, handling order edits. Often phones with customers about ETAs.

A typical day: Inbox of orders to process — some from the Phone Team, some from the website, some from outside reps. Edge cases (back-orders, partial shipments, payment issues) take up half the time.

Top jobs to be done

  • Process orders to shipped without re-keying anything.
  • When an order has a problem (back-order, payment, address), flag it to the Sales Rep and to the buyer.
  • Generate invoices and packing slips with one click.

What they own

Full on Orders & Returns. Read on accounts, catalog, pricing, shipping.

Not their job

Configuring shipping rules. Setting prices. Catalog. Marketing.

Success looks like

Avg order processing time <5 min; invoice generation 100% automated; <1% of orders need re-work.

Frustration today: Re-keys data between WC, the shipping plugin, and Webgility. Same data, three places.
Shipping & Warehouse Lead Floor side · includes warehouse picking Customer-side

Who they really are: Floor-based, on the move. Often has a tablet or scanner gun. Manages pickers (where they exist) and the carrier handoff.

A typical day: Pick lists, pack orders, print labels, hand off to carrier. Handle exceptions (oversize, dangerous goods, overseas).

Top jobs to be done

  • Get today's orders out the door correctly, with the right rate and the right label.
  • When an unusual order comes in (oversized, hazmat, international), see the special handling required.
  • Reconcile yesterday's shipments with carrier confirmations.

What they own

Shipping configuration. Warehouse floor execution. Returns workflow. Carrier handoff.

Not their job

Order entry. Pricing. Marketing. Bookkeeping.

Success looks like

99%+ same-day-ship rate for orders received before cutoff; <1% mis-pick rate; shipping costs forecasted accurately.

Frustration today: Five plugins overlap on shipping rates with no documented precedence. They're never sure what rate the customer paid until after the fact.
Bookkeeper / Finance May be in-house OR external accounting firm Customer-side

Who they really are: Often part-time in-house, sometimes an external accounting firm. AR/AP focused. Spreadsheet and QuickBooks-heavy.

A typical day: Sync orders to QuickBooks, post payments, reconcile bank deposits, follow up on past-due AR, prepare tax reports.

Top jobs to be done

  • Reconcile the month without surprises.
  • When a customer is past due, follow up and (if approved) put their account on hold.
  • Generate tax reports for the accountant's quarterly filings.

What they own

Read on orders, accounts, pricing, returns. Write on payment adjustments and credit memos. External firms get an auditable, revocable login.

Not their job

Catalog. Marketing. Site ops. Order entry. Shipping.

Success looks like

Month-end close <3 business days; AR > 60 days reduced quarter over quarter; zero QB-sync errors.

Frustration today: Webgility errors require a developer to debug; no visibility into "is this customer creditworthy."
02

Handoffs & Interfaces — Where Roles Meet

Real work is collaborative. The redesign isn't just about giving each role its own swim lane; it's about making the moments WHERE roles hand off to each other clean and visible. Here are the ten most important interfaces — what triggers them, who's involved, what's exchanged, and how the new admin should change the experience.

01
Trigger · New lead arrives
Lead → Sales Rep assignment
Marketing Coordinator → Sales Director → Sales Rep
Exchanged: Lead record + initial context + rep assignment + intake notes.
Today: Email or spreadsheet. Often falls through the cracks.
New: Shared lead inbox visible to Marketing, Sales Director, Regional Manager.
02
Trigger · Rep places contract-priced order
Custom price flows to the rep
Sales Director / Regional Mgr → Sales Rep
Exchanged: Customer-specific price list + boundaries (cannot go below threshold).
Today: Reps memorize or call manager. Pricing errors common.
New: Pricing visible automatically when rep opens that buyer's order screen.
03
Trigger · Promotion goes live
Coupon publishing
Marketing Coordinator → Phone Team Sales Rep
Exchanged: Coupon code + eligibility rules + start/end date.
Today: Email blast to team. Reps forget. Buyers ask, reps caught off-guard.
New: Active promos surfaced on rep order screen and Phone Team workspace.
04
Trigger · Order has a fulfillment problem
Order issue → rep → buyer
Order Processor / Shipping Lead → Sales Rep → Buyer
Exchanged: Problem detail + suggested resolution + rep approval needed.
Today: Email or Slack. Sometimes forgotten.
New: Account flag visible on rep dashboard with "action required" status.
05
Trigger · Stock out during order entry
Inventory shortage in real time
Catalog Manager → Sales Rep / Phone Team
Exchanged: Stock-on-hand + ETA for restock + alternative SKUs.
Today: Stale inventory data; reps promise delivery they can't deliver.
New: Live inventory in rep order flow; suggested alternatives on stockout.
06
Trigger · Discount above manager threshold
Pricing exception → Owner approval
Sales Director / Regional Mgr → Owner / Administrator
Exchanged: Customer + ask + margin impact + business justification.
Today: Phone call or email. Often delayed.
New: Approval queue on Owner dashboard with SLA tracking.
07
Trigger · New rep joins the team
Rep onboarding workflow
Sales Director → Owner / Administrator (activates) → Sales Rep (joins)
Exchanged: Account creation + region assignment + customer assignments + training resources.
Today: Multiple touch points; takes days to set up.
New: Single workflow — "add a rep" → assign + activate + onboard in one motion.
08
Trigger · Buyer calls with non-order question
Customer service ticket lifecycle
Phone Team / Sales Rep → Order Processor / Shipping → back to caller
Exchanged: Ticket + buyer + issue + resolution.
Today: Ad-hoc; tickets get lost.
New: Ticket queue visible in Returns & Tickets area; assignable, trackable.
09
Trigger · Account becomes past-due
AR follow-up loop
Bookkeeper → Sales Rep → Buyer
Exchanged: Past-due amount + payment terms + suggested action (call, hold, write-off).
Today: Email; rep often unaware until customer is 90+ days overdue.
New: AR-aging visible on rep dashboard; auto-flag at 30/60/90.
10
Trigger · New SKUs ready to launch
New product launch coordination
Catalog Manager Marketing Coordinator → Phone Team / Sales Rep
Exchanged: New SKU list + selling points + launch promo.
Today: Email blast; reps don't always read.
New: "What's new this month" surfaced in rep daily snapshot.
03

Rollout & Change Plan

A system-wide admin redesign is the highest-blast-radius change a SaaS can make. Going live with a "big bang" launch is the worst available path. Here's a phased rollout that minimizes disruption, validates the design with real customers, and lets Built2Buy build muscle before facing every customer at once.

Phase 1 · Internal first
Built2Buy uses it on internal sandbox sites
2-3 weeks
Built2Buy's own staff use the new admin to manage internal sandbox sites. Surfaces UX bugs before any customer sees them, builds support muscle, generates the runbook that customer-support staff will need.
Who: Platform Operators, internal QA. Goal: Find the obvious problems while no customer is watching.
Phase 2 · Pilot customers
3-5 hand-picked customer sites
4 weeks
Hand-picked sites: include at least one large customer with role specialization, one small customer where one person does everything, and one mid-size in between. Account managers run weekly check-ins. Goal: validate that the role model works for both Karen-the-everything-person AND for a 30-person dealer.
Who: 3-5 selected customers + their account managers. Goal: Validate role model across customer sizes; iterate fast.
Phase 3 · Parallel-run, opt-in
Both old and new admin available
4-6 weeks
Opt-in. Both old and new admin available simultaneously; customer chooses default. Built2Buy publishes which percentage of pilot customers stayed on new vs. went back. Peer pressure as marketing rather than mandate.
Who: Self-selecting customers + account managers. Goal: Build evidence base; refine onboarding.
Phase 4 · General rollout
New is default; switch-back toggle visible
8-12 weeks
New admin is default for new customers. Default-on for existing customers with a "switch back" toggle visible for 90 days. Track who uses the toggle and engage account managers to find out why.
Who: All customers. Goal: Move 90%+ to the new admin without forcing.
Phase 5 · Old admin sunset
Hard date for retirement
Announced month 6, executed month 9
Hard date, communicated relentlessly, with white-glove migration help for any customer still on the old view at month 8. Never roll a customer over during their seasonal peak.
Who: Account managers + remaining holdouts. Goal: Clean retirement of the old admin.

Top 5 risks to manage before launch

  1. The "Karen problem" sinks adoption if multi-role assignment isn't shipped on day one. Single-person admin is the modal user at small customers; the role-specialized model assumes a workforce most customers don't have.
  2. Built2Buy's own support team is the hidden bottleneck. If they aren't trained and runbook-ready before the first customer sees the new admin, every support call becomes a confidence-eroding event.
  3. Classic-mode toggle is necessary but dangerous. Without it the rollout risks churn; with it past 9 months you've created permanent technical debt and a two-UI support burden.
  4. Custom-role migration is the data-loss risk nobody is watching. Customers who customized their permissions via third-party plugins will silently lose configuration unless someone audits each site pre-migration.
  5. The integrator/consultant stakeholder is missing from the design and will become the loudest external critic. They influence multiple customers, see the change repeatedly, and have no formal seat at the table. Add them before launch, not after.

Success metrics — watch these at 30/60/90 days post-launch

  • Time-to-task for the top 10 workflows. Measured pre/post. Anything that got slower is a design failure.
  • Support ticket volume by category. Spike in "where is X" tickets past month 2 = navigation isn't intuitive.
  • Classic-mode toggle usage per customer. High and sticky usage at month 3 = unmet need.
  • Role-assignment churn in the first 90 days. High churn = the role model is wrong for that customer.
  • Feature adoption of net-new functional areas (Returns & Tickets, Promotions split). Flat adoption = the surface isn't selling itself.
